System for Detection and Track Treatment Process for Vascular Dementia

ResearchProjectScope

Literature Survey

Dementia, particularly vascular dementia, is a progressive neurological disorder affecting memory, thinking, and daily activities, accounting for 20% of dementia cases. It results from reduced blood flow to the brain, often caused by strokes or cardiovascular conditions. Traditional diagnostic tools like the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) have limitations, especially in detecting early cognitive impairment. These methods often provide only a snapshot of cognitive function and lack continuous monitoring, which is crucial for effective disease management. Recent technological advances, particularly in machine learning and convolutional neural networks (CNNs), offer promising solutions. CNNs can analyze speech patterns associated with cognitive decline, achieving over 85% accuracy in early dementia diagnosis. By integrating these technologies with real-time monitoring, healthcare providers can tailor treatments, improving patient outcomes and reducing healthcare costs. This shift toward personalized care is crucial for better management of dementia and enhancing the quality of life for patients.

Research Problem

Dementia is a disease that impairs the ability to remember, think, or make decisions and interferes with everyday activities. The second most typical form of dementia is vascular dementia. It is caused by issues with the blood flow to the brain. Vascular dementia can have a sudden onset, with symptoms appearing suddenly after a stroke or a series of small strokes. The progression of vascular dementia can be more variable than that of other diseases. In some cases, the symptoms of vascular dementia can improve with treatment, but in many other cases, the symptoms will persist and may worsen over time. The treatment of vascular dementia can vary depending on the underlying cause of blood vessel damage and may include medications, lifestyle changes, rehabilitation, and surgery. The diagnosis of vascular dementia can be more challenging than the diagnosis of other types of dementia diseases, as it requires a comprehensive evaluation of medical history, symptoms, and test results, including imaging tests and blood tests. The easiest way to identify if a person is normal or atypical is with a simple questionnaire. There are a few tests that are used, such as the Neuropsychiatry Unit Cognitive Assessment Tool (NUCAG) and the Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE), that give patients a series of questions and tasks to determine their various cognitive functions. Most of them are still happening as a manual process. A doctor or a nurse would ask the questions and assign the tasks, and based on the patient's responses, they would assign a suitable grade. If this process were automated, the data collected could be used for a variety of other purposes. Also, each patient's efficient treatment process can be different from one to another, and finding out which one of them is which can be easier with those tests too..

Predict risk of dementia through ability to follow simple commands and Emotion analysis.

The use of command-following abilities to identify dementia through advanced technologies is relatively new compared to other methods. It has the potential to bring new levels of accuracy, efficiency, and accessibility to the detection of dementia. An accurate marking system that could help doctors make more precise diagnoses and treatment decisions based on patients' command-following abilities, language skills, and understanding can be considered unique. In addition to these abilities, we have incorporated emotion detection during the command-following tests. Since the emotions of dementia patients can fluctuate continuously, monitoring their emotional responses provides valuable insights into their cognitive state. This integration further enhances the system's ability to offer personalized care by considering both cognitive and emotional aspects, leading to more comprehensive and accurate dementia diagnosis and management.

Clock Drawing test to assess drawing and memorizing ability of patients

Output will offer feedback to the doctor on their patients' performance, highlighting areas where they are strong and areas where they need to improve, and it will be useful for quickly identifying the user's level of proficiency. From this type of implementation, I hope it will be helpful for doctors to make more accurate diagnoses and treatment decisions.

Categorizing a person as normal or having a risk of suffering from dementia based on speech

Use of keyword analysis as a method to determine if patients with dementia are having difficulty remembering certain words or concepts. The idea is that changes in language abilities can be an early indicator of cognitive decline in patients with dementia, and by analyzing their speech for difficulty remembering specific words, it may be possible to detect these changes and potentially diagnose dementia at an early stage.



Predict risk of dementia through writing skills and differentiating patients as dementia or depression

The collected samples would undergo analysis through automated methods, including the application of machine learning algorithms, to detect temporal changes in patients' cognitive abilities. These algorithms would compare the current samples against historical data to identify any variations or decline in skills. If the total marks from prior assessments fall within a middle range (45-65), the system would differentiate between dementia and depression based on a predefined set of questionnaire responses. This approach enables the continuous monitoring of cognitive health and provides a diagnostic distinction between the two conditions.

Methodology

The goal of this research project is to integrate traditional cognitive tests with modern machine learning techniques to develop a complete diagnostic system for vascular dementia. The methodology focuses on analyzing the physical and emotional reactions of patients during cognitive tasks, leveraging audio and video data. The system is designed to reliably identify and track cognitive decline, particularly in dementia patients.

The first phase involves collecting data through a series of tasks aimed at assessing motor, cognitive, and emotional abilities. High-definition audio and video recordings are captured as patients complete tasks such as following commands, recalling number sequences, and demonstrating cognitive flexibility. To ensure consistency, video data, especially from hand gesture tasks, is preprocessed by shrinking images to 120x320 pixels, converting them to grayscale, and normalizing pixel values between 0 and 1. Data augmentation techniques such as random rotations, zooms, and horizontal flips are applied to increase model robustness. This preprocessing pipeline ensures high-quality data is provided to the machine learning models for training.

For audio data, preprocessing includes segmenting continuous audio streams into discrete responses, volume leveling, and noise reduction using techniques like spectral gating. Speech task text is tokenized, normalized (converted to lowercase, and stripped of punctuation), and padded or truncated to meet the machine learning models’ input requirements.

Data Collection

In the initial phase, data were collected through a series of tasks designed to assess participants' motor, cognitive, and emotional abilities. High-definition video and audio recordings captured participants' responses as they followed commands, recalled numerical sequences, and performed cognitive flexibility tasks. The video data, especially from hand gesture tasks, were preprocessed by shrinking each image to 120x320 pixels, converting it to grayscale, and normalizing pixel values to a range of 0 to 1. Data augmentation techniques, such as random rotations, zooms, and horizontal flips, were applied to increase the resilience of the model. Similarly, audio data were segmented into discrete segments, with volume leveling and noise reduction techniques like spectral gating applied. Speech tasks were tokenized and normalized by converting text to lowercase and removing punctuation, ensuring that the inputs met machine learning requirements.

Developing the ML model

The diagnostic system leverages a CNN-based framework for analyzing both audio and visual data. The CNN architecture was designed to accommodate various input formats, including audio spectrograms for speech tasks and images from hand gesture tasks. Multiple convolutional layers with 'relu' activation functions were applied to reduce the dimensionality of feature maps without losing important characteristics. These 3D feature maps were flattened into 1D vectors, processed by dense layers to capture intricate patterns, and classified using a softmax function into predefined categories, such as hand motions or emotional states.

For tasks involving speech and memory assessments, the model architecture incorporated spectrograms processed through convolutional layers. Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) architectures, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) or Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), were used to capture temporal dependencies in the data during tasks that required sequence recall. The final dense layers classified the correctness and fluency of the participants' responses. The system was trained using a dataset split into training, validation, and test sets with a 70-20-10 ratio. Techniques like early stopping, model checkpointing, and K-fold cross-validation were employed to prevent overfitting and ensure generalization.

Output

The system evaluated patients through controlled tasks, including command-following, where patients replicated hand gestures shown on a screen. The system analyzed the precision of the gestures and the time taken to execute them, providing a numerical score for motor and cognitive functions. Emotional evaluations were also conducted by detecting facial expression changes during task execution.

Tasks such as the clock drawing test assessed spatial orientation and cognitive flexibility by analyzing the accuracy of clock drawings. The numerical sequence task tested memory recall by requiring participants to input sequences forward and backward, with task difficulty adjusted in real-time based on performance.

In speech and memory assessments, the system measured verbal fluency by asking participants to list items in a category. Machine learning models evaluated the correctness and fluency of their responses. To differentiate between dementia and depression, the system analyzed handwritten samples, using CNNs to detect differences in letter alignment, pressure, and formation. These indicators helped distinguish between psychomotor retardation, typical of depression, and motor control issues, common in dementia.

Key performance metrics, including accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, were calculated to assess the system's performance. Confusion matrices provided insights into the types of errors made, allowing for model improvement. Hyperparameter optimization was conducted using grid and random search techniques to fine-tune the model for optimal performance.
